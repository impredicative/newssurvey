Additionally, following a balanced diet with optimal nutrient intake is linked to neuroplasticity and brain health, which are crucial in managing anxiety and mood regulation[1,2,3,4,5,6].

3. Herbal Treatments for Anxiety

Herbal treatments have gained considerable attention as potential alternatives to conventional medications for anxiety management. Chamomile, for instance, is widely consumed as tea and acts as a mild sedative and muscle relaxant. Passionflower and valerian are noted for their sedative properties, with valerian also functioning as a muscle relaxant. Gotu kola is recognized for cognitive enhancement and mood disorder reduction, and lemon balm is utilized for its calming effects on stress and sleep disturbances[36].

Saffron, a spice known for its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory attributes, has demonstrated potential in treating anxiety and depressive symptoms in clinical trials. Notably, an 8-week double-blind trial revealed mood improvements in participants who consumed saffron extract compared to those given a placebo. Saffron's ability to enhance neurotransmitter production offers a promising addition to an integrated mental health treatment plan. However, the high cost and variable quality necessitate the use of standardized tablets to ensure effective outcomes[37].

Ashwagandha, a significant herb in Ayurvedic medicine, has been highlighted for its sleep-inducing properties, linking it to potential anxiety management. Research identified triethylene glycol (TEG) in ashwagandha leaves as the active component promoting non-rapid eye movement (NREM) sleep, which is crucial for individuals experiencing anxiety and related sleep disorders such as insomnia[38]. Similarly, adaptogens like American and Asian ginseng, Rhodiola rosea, and chaga are believed to help the body manage stress by restoring balance. However, their effectiveness might diminish over extended use due to resistance, and further research is necessary to establish optimal dosages and long-term safety[39].

Kratom users report using it for anxiety relief, among other purposes. The compound, sharing a relation with coffee, acts on brain opiate receptors to enhance mood. Despite its potential, kratom remains unregulated by the FDA, raising concerns about safety and product quality. Surveys indicate a low incidence of severe substance use disorder associated with kratom, yet potential adverse effects, such as hallucinations and liver damage, highlight the need for caution and professional advice prior to use[40].

Lavender, available as an oral supplement or through aromatherapy, holds preliminary evidence of anxiety reduction. It is generally well-tolerated but could cause side effects like constipation and headaches. The potential interactions with sedative medications necessitate careful consideration of its use. Lemon balm also shows potential in easing nervousness and excitability but may induce nausea and stomach discomfort in some individuals[41,42].

Despite the potential benefits, users must be aware that herbal supplements are not as rigorously tested or regulated as conventional medications, leading to variability in quality and safety. Consulting with healthcare providers is crucial to minimize risks, particularly when using herbal remedies alongside other medications. Further research is essential to fully comprehend the long-term effects and mechanisms of these herbal treatments to ensure their safe and effective use in managing anxiety[41,42].

Research has consistently highlighted the benefits of omega-3 supplementation in reducing anxiety levels in various populations, including adolescents, adults, and those with specific health conditions[48,29,49,23].

Omega-3 fatty acids have also been identified to alleviate anxiety through their ability to reduce the production of pro-inflammatory cytokines. Moreover, diets deficient in omega-3s during crucial stages, such as adolescence and pregnancy, are linked to increased anxiety and may highlight the necessity for these nutrients during these times[48,52].

The impact of omega-3 fatty acids on anxiety is supported by recent clinical guidelines that underscore the benefits of EPA and DHA in managing anxiety disorders. Despite these positive outcomes, researchers stress the need for increased dietary intake of omega-3-rich foods over supplements to achieve better overall health outcomes, suggesting a more integrated approach involving lifestyle and diet changes[1,49].

The emerging evidence solidifies omega-3 fatty acids' role in reducing anxiety symptoms through their anti-inflammatory properties and influence on neurotransmitter regulation. The relationship between the Mediterranean diet and mental health is supported by numerous research findings. For instance, a study from the University of South Australia found that adherence to this dietary pattern led to significant reductions in anxiety and stress among older adults, regardless of other demographic variables like age and BMI. This is in part due to key components of the diet, such as high consumption of fruits, nuts, legumes, and limited intake of sugar-sweetened beverages[4]. Furthermore, a systematic review involving over 32,000 adults indicated that those adhering to a Mediterranean diet were less likely to develop depression, a condition often comorbid with anxiety[6].

The diet also supports gut health, fostering the growth of beneficial bacteria that play a role in emotional regulation and mental well-being through the gut-brain axis. By promoting a healthy microbiota through the consumption of fiber-rich foods and healthy fats, the Mediterranean diet helps in managing anxiety symptoms by maintaining a balanced gut environment, thus influencing mood and cognitive functions positively[53,28].

Moreover, the diet's emphasis on low glycemic index foods helps stabilize blood sugar levels, which is particularly beneficial for mood regulation and anxiety management. Foods with a low glycemic index prevent drastic fluctuations in blood sugar that can lead to mood disturbances, thereby potentially reducing anxiety symptoms[33].

The Mediterranean diet also plays a significant role in modulating metabolic health, reducing the prevalence of metabolic disorders and inflammation, both of which can affect neural circuits linked to mood and emotions. The consumption of monounsaturated fats, particularly olive oil, is a significant component associated with these benefits[15].

In conclusion, the Mediterranean diet's balanced composition of macronutrients and micronutrients, alongside its anti-inflammatory and gut-supporting properties, make it a suitable dietary approach for the management of anxiety. 10. The Impact of Sugar and Processed Foods on Anxiety

Recent data strongly indicate a significant link between the consumption of sugar and processed foods and the exacerbation of anxiety symptoms. Diets high in added sugars and refined carbohydrates lead to rapid fluctuations in blood sugar levels which can result in mood instability and heightened anxiety. This effect stems from the body's response to the oscillation between hyperglycemia and hypoglycemia, which stimulates stress hormones such as adrenaline. Such hormonal shifts can increase feelings of anxiety and irritability, particularly in individuals with predisposed anxiety disorders[33,2].

The consumption of ultra-processed foods, which are typically high in added sugars, saturated fats, and salt, has been correlated with increased risk of adverse mental health outcomes, including anxiety and depression. Studies have found that individuals who consume a large amount of ultra-processed foods report more days affected by anxiety and mental health issues than those who consume these foods less[14,17,16]. These foods lack essential nutrients and fiber which are critical for maintaining mental well-being, further contributing to their negative impact on mental health[14].

Metabolic changes induced by diets high in sugar and processed foods, such as increased inflammation and hormonal imbalances, have also been implicated in the development of anxiety disorders. For example, an increased intake of sugars can provoke inflammation in brain areas associated with mood regulation, contributing to the development of anxiodepressive symptoms. These inflammatory responses are notably distinct between sexes, suggesting potential differences in susceptibility and impacts of sugar consumption on anxiety between males and females[15,32].

Moreover, research has shown that psychological responses to stress, such as emotional eating, lead individuals to consume more processed and sugary foods as a way of coping with stressors. This behavior perpetuates a cycle where the temporary relief from anxiety achieved through consuming comfort foods ultimately results in a rebound effect, worsening anxiety symptoms in the long term[66,67,68].

The relationship between sugar and processed food intake, hormonal imbalances, and mood regulation suggests that reducing these dietary components can be beneficial for mental health. Alternatives such as the Mediterranean diet, which emphasizes fruits, vegetables, whole grains, and healthy fats, have shown to alleviate anxiety symptoms while providing the nutrients necessary for maintaining a balanced mood and overall mental health[4,53].

Further investigation into the specific biological mechanisms by which sugars and processed foods influence anxiety could support more targeted dietary interventions. The current body of research highlights the necessity of dietary modifications as both a preventive and therapeutic strategy for managing anxiety and related mental health disorders[5,23].

Not all high-fat diets are equal, and the type of fat consumed significantly affects mental health outcomes. Unsaturated fats, like those found in walnuts and other sources of omega-3 fatty acids, have been noted for their beneficial effects on brain health, contrasting with the negative implications of diets high in saturated and trans fats[74,75,5,22].

Current evidence necessitates further exploration to fully understand the intricate relationship between high-fat diets and anxiety. Overall, the cognitive and behavioral impacts of nutrients elucidate the intricate connections between diet and mental health. Nutritional interventions, including omega-3 fatty acids, B vitamins, probiotics, antioxidants, and fiber, hold promise in managing anxiety and enhancing cognitive and emotional well-being. As research progresses, it becomes increasingly evident that nutrition must be considered a foundational component of mental health strategies[1,23,81,74].

14. Lifestyle and Nutritional Interventions for Anxiety

Lifestyle and nutritional interventions are pivotal in managing anxiety, with a comprehensive approach involving dietary patterns, exercise, stress management, and sleep hygiene. Dietary choices directly impact mental health, with specific foods and nutrients having profound effects on mood regulation and anxiety.

Dietary interventions involving whole foods, particularly those rich in complex carbohydrates, omega-3 fatty acids, and B vitamins, can stabilize mood through their roles in neurotransmitter production and inflammatory response modulation. Complex carbohydrates from whole grains and vegetables release glucose slowly, maintaining energy levels and reducing mood swings[2]. Omega-3 fatty acids, abundant in fish and nuts, support brain health and communication between neurons, while B vitamins like B6 and B12 are involved in synthesizing neurotransmitters integral for mood stabilization[2,74,1].

The Mediterranean diet, which includes high intake of fruits, vegetables, nuts, whole grains, and olive oil, has been associated with improved mental health outcomes, reducing anxiety and stress. This diet emphasizes minimally processed foods rich in essential nutrients and antioxidants, which combat oxidative stress and inflammation, common underlying factors of anxiety and depression[4,6,27].

Interventions focusing on gut health through prebiotics, probiotics, and fiber-rich diets further illustrate the gut-brain connection's role in anxiety management. Probiotics in fermented foods can enhance gut microbiota diversity, influencing emotional regulation and stress response pathways positively[53,82,83]. Fiber-rich diets are essential for producing short-chain fatty acids, which support gut barrier integrity and reduce stress-induced inflammation[54,69].

Exercise is another critical component of anxiety management, with research showing its effectiveness in reducing symptoms. Regular physical activity stimulates endorphin production and enhances mood while reducing stress hormone levels. It can also improve cognitive functions and mood regulation[84,85].

Moreover, mindfulness practices and meditation are increasingly recognized for their ability to reduce stress and anxiety by fostering emotional regulation and resilience. Mindfulness meditation has been shown to lower physiological stress markers, aiding in overall stress management and mental health improvement[86].

Sleep quality is intrinsically linked to anxiety; improving sleep patterns through consistent sleep routines and creating conducive sleep environments can have significant anxiolytic effects[87,88].

In summary, a tailored approach integrating nutrition, exercise, mindfulness, and sleep hygiene forms a comprehensive strategy for managing anxiety. These interventions address the biological, psychological, and sociological factors that contribute to mental health, underscoring the need for holistic health practices in anxiety management[2,4,84,27].

15. Potential Risks and Ethical Considerations in Supplement Use for Anxiety

The use of dietary supplements for managing anxiety brings with it several potential risks and ethical considerations. A primary concern revolves around the lack of regulation in the supplement industry, which often results in products on the market that are not thoroughly vetted for safety or efficacy. For instance, the FDA classifies supplements as food rather than drugs, allowing manufacturers to bypass proving their products' effectiveness before reaching consumers. This regulatory gap exposes individuals to possible misinformation and undisclosed harmful components in supplements marketed for weight loss, muscle gain, or anxiety relief[89].

Additionally, specific supplements such as melatonin have been shown to be inaccurately labeled, with dosages considerably exceeding those advertised, which has resulted in an increase in poison center calls primarily involving children[90]. Such discrepancies underscore the dangers of using supplements without appropriate labeling or dosage scrutiny, increasing the risk of overdose and unwanted side effects.

Moreover, there are ethical considerations concerning the societal and psychological impacts of supplement use. Some supplements, such as vitamin and mineral boosters, could encourage individuals to self-medicate rather than seek professional help, potentially masking underlying mental health issues that might be better addressed through comprehensive medical treatment and lifestyle changes[18]. The presence of socioeconomic barriers also raises equity issues, considering that access to high-quality supplements may not be equal across different populations, potentially widening health disparities[74].

There's also a risk of herb-drug interactions. Supplements like kratom are commonly used for anxiety despite having significant risk profiles, including severe adverse effects and a potential for addiction. The lack of FDA approval for many such products leaves consumers unprotected, further highlighting the ethical need for stronger regulatory practices[91,40]. Additionally, the promotion of supplements like CBD without robust evidence backing their efficacy and safety, particularly in children, poses ethical concerns about informed decision-making and the possibility of false hope being sold to vulnerable individuals and families[92,93].

The potential exists for supplements like omega-3 fatty acids and probiotics to benefit mental health, but relying solely on these without traditional treatments could deride the utility of evidence-based medicine, potentially leading to suboptimal patient outcomes[49,94]. There is also a risk of perpetuating a mindset where natural equates to safe, despite evidence that even natural supplements can have significant adverse effects or interact with prescribed medications, creating serious health risks. Thus, ethical practice demands healthcare providers provide balanced, evidence-based advice regarding the use of supplements through open discussions with their patients to weigh potential benefits against associated risks and to consider personalized health needs and comprehensive treatment strategies[23,95].

Ultimately, to ensure safe and effective use, supplementation for anxiety should be approached with caution, guided by evidence-based medicine, and integrated ethically within broader mental health management strategies to mitigate potential risks and adhere to the principles of informed consent and patient autonomy[81,1].

16. Future Directions in Research on Nutrition and Anxiety

The future of research on nutrition and anxiety calls for a broad and integrated exploration of various dietary interventions, mechanisms influencing mental health, and the underlying biological systems involved. Several key areas show promising potential for future investigation, informed by current findings.

First, the role of dietary patterns such as the Mediterranean diet, known for its beneficial effects on physical and mental health, should be further explored. This diet emphasizes whole foods rich in omega-3 fatty acids, antioxidants, and fiber, which may contribute to reduced anxiety symptoms by modulating inflammation and supporting healthy gut microbiota[6,47,96]. Future research should aim to establish the causal relationships between these dietary components and their specific effects on anxiety and mood regulation.

Additionally, omega-3 fatty acids, essential in brain health but not produced by the body, remain a focal point for understanding their impact on anxiety. Research should extend beyond prenatal and postnatal benefits to encompass broader anxiety conditions across various populations[97,49,22]. Investigations into the optimal doses, long-term effects, and potential integration with other dietary elements will be vital.

There is also growing interest in the microbiota-gut-brain axis and how manipulating gut health through probiotics, prebiotics, and specific dietary interventions can alleviate anxiety[98,83,12]. Future studies should delve into identifying which gut bacterial strains have the most significant impact on mental health and how altering microbial diversity through diet influences anxiety symptoms.

Moreover, the examination of intermittent fasting and time-restricted eating patterns offers another avenue of interest, as these approaches have been linked to improved mood and cognitive functions through their effects on metabolic health and the gut-brain axis[99,100]. Further research should quantify these effects and explore their implications for anxiety management.

Micronutrients, such as B vitamins and vitamin D, have demonstrated potential efficacy in reducing anxiety symptoms and warrant expanded investigation into mechanisms, optimal supplementation protocols, and interactions with broader dietary patterns[43,44,21].

Research should not overlook the importance of macronutrient balance, especially concerning high-fat diets that may exacerbate anxiety through inflammatory pathways and altered neurochemical mechanisms[58,57,73]. Future studies could enhance our understanding of how different types of dietary fats—saturated vs. unsaturated—affect mental health outcomes.

Finally, integrative approaches combining dietary interventions with behavioral and psychological strategies show promise in yielding comprehensive benefits for individuals with anxiety, indicating opportunities for developing multi-component interventions tailored to individual needs[101,102,25].

Overall, future research must employ rigorous, well-designed studies that account for individual and group differences, including age, gender, genetic predispositions, and lifestyle factors, to build a robust framework for personalized nutritional strategies in anxiety management. Through collaboration between nutritionists, mental health professionals, and genetic researchers, a more holistic understanding of how diet impacts anxiety can lead to optimized, evidence-based treatment strategies for enhancing mental well-being across various populations[103,31,104].
