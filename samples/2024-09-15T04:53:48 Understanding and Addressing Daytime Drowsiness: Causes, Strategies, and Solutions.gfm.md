## <a id="section-1"></a>1. Introduction to Daytime Drowsiness and Sleep Patterns

Daytime drowsiness is a widespread issue affecting a significant portion of the global population. Research consistently shows that numerous factors contribute to this condition, prominently including inadequate nighttime sleep and various sleep disorders. Many adults fail to meet the recommended seven to nine hours of sleep per night, which can lead to the accumulation of sleep debt and a pronounced tendency toward daytime drowsiness[^1].

The complexity of sleep patterns is evident from studies that explore how the body's natural mechanism attempts to compensate for sleep loss. For instance, cyclical variations in sleep duration, as noted in research, indicate that even slight disruptions in the regularity of sleep patterns can lead to a sense of continuous fatigue and the body's attempts to regain equilibrium through extended sleep episodes or naps[^2]. The concept of 'sleep debt' is crucial here, as any deficiency in nighttime sleep may necessitate compensatory strategies like extended napping, though this might not be as effective in fully restoring wakefulness[^3].

Additionally, genetic factors play a role in how individuals cope with sleep deprivation and daytime drowsiness. Certain genetic mutations can allow some individuals to maintain function despite shorter sleep durations, suggesting a biological basis for sleep requirements and the management of sleep loss[^4]. However, for the majority, insufficient sleep contributes to a host of cognitive deficits, including impaired memory and concentration, which often manifest as excessive daytime sleepiness[^5].

Further complicating this scenario are lifestyle factors and societal behaviors, such as increased screentime before bed, irregular sleep schedules, and shift work, all of which can disrupt normal circadian rhythms and lead to sleep fragmentation[^6][^3][^7]. Adults over the age of 40, a demographic frequently highlighted in sleep studies, tend to experience a decline in sleep efficiency, which can exacerbate daytime grogginess and the perceived need for additional rest during the day[^8][^9].

Physical health also significantly influences daytime drowsiness. Conditions such as obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) and other sleep-disordered breathing conditions are major contributors, often undiagnosed, leading to fragmented sleep and subsequent daytime fatigue[^10]. Cardiovascular and metabolic health issues linked to short sleep duration further highlight the importance of adequate sleep[^11][^12].

In conclusion, the intricacy of factors contributing to daytime drowsiness underscores the critical importance of addressing sleep quality and duration within the broader context of individual health and lifestyle. In terms of interventions, improving sleep hygiene through regular sleep schedules, minimizing exposure to electronic screens before bed, and ensuring a conducive sleep environment are recommended strategies to enhance nighttime rest and reduce the reliance on naps[^13]. Addressing underlying medical conditions such as sleep apnea with appropriate interventions like CPAP therapy can also significantly improve sleep quality and thereby lessen daytime drowsiness[^10][^14]. By understanding the multitude of influences on sleep, individuals can better manage daytime alertness and reduce the health implications associated with extensive sleep debt and chronic fatigue.

## <a id="section-2"></a>2. Causes and Prevalence of Excessive Daytime Sleepiness in Adults

Other sleep disorders like insomnia and narcolepsy also play a crucial role, where insomnia can manifest as difficulty in falling asleep, disrupting sleep continuity, and leading to impaired daytime functioning[^29][^30]. Narcolepsy, a less common but severe sleep disorder, involves sudden and uncontrollable sleep episodes and can significantly impact daily activities[^23][^31].

Lifestyle choices and habits, including dietary intake, sleep hygiene, and physical activity level, are influential in the prevalence of EDS among adults[^21][^32][^33][^34][^35]. Insufficient sleep, chronic insomnia, and inconsistent sleep schedules contribute to a vicious cycle of sleep debt, prompting reliance on daytime naps[^38][^39][^40].

Medical conditions such as depression, diabetes, obesity, and cardiovascular diseases are also significant contributors to EDS[^25][^12][^41][^42]. These health issues are often interconnected with sleep disorders, creating a compounded effect that worsens daytime drowsiness[^12][^43][^32]. Obstructive sleep apnea, for example, is associated with increased risk factors like metabolic syndrome and cardiovascular mortality, particularly when left untreated[^44][^45].

The prevalence of EDS is high, with studies indicating that a significant portion of the adult population reports daytime sleepiness, often without an apparent cause[^46][^47]. Environmental factors, such as exposure to artificial light before bedtime, can disrupt circadian rhythms, leading to delayed sleep onset and morning drowsiness[^48][^49]. To address EDS effectively, it is essential to consider the multifactorial nature of its causes. ## <a id="section-3"></a>3. The Role of Sleep Duration and Sleep Debt in Daytime Alertness

Sleep duration is a fundamental determinant of daytime alertness, with insufficient sleep leading to sleep debt—a cumulation of unmet sleep needs that can impair physical and cognitive function during waking hours. The recommended sleep duration for adults typically falls between seven to nine hours per night, which is considered essential for maintaining optimal health and performance. When individuals frequently do not meet this requirement, they accrue sleep debt, manifesting as daytime drowsiness and impaired alertness[^4][^9][^56].

Studies have consistently linked short sleep durations, defined as less than six hours per night, with increased risks of impaired cognitive performance, mood disturbances, and a variety of health issues including obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and even mortality[^57][^58][^59]. Such short sleep durations are associated with excessive daytime sleepiness, highlighting the significance of sleep debt and its ability to impair critical aspects of health and daily functioning[^5][^60][^61][^62].

Conversely, sleep durations extending beyond nine hours may also pose health risks, potentially leading to states of prolonged sedation and increased risks of similar health conditions. This indicates that both insufficient and excessive sleep may be detrimental, underscoring the need for balanced sleep duration [^63][^64][^65]. The effects are often exacerbated by disrupted sleep patterns, which are commonly due to irregular sleep schedules or sleep disorders such as obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), which fragments sleep and contributes to significant sleep debt[^2][^58][^66].

Moreover, the concept of social jetlag—discrepancies between weekday and weekend sleep patterns—further complicates sleep health and exacerbates sleep debt, leading to greater daytime drowsiness and associated risks[^3][^67][^68]. Addressing sleep debt requires not only achieving adequate nightly sleep duration but also consistency in sleep timing, which helps regulate circadian rhythms and minimizes misalignment that can contribute to sleep debt[^38][^32].

Sleep debt is not easily remedied by simply increasing sleep on weekends; rather, consistent sleep patterns across all days of the week are advised to recover from chronic sleep deprivation[^68][^69]. To mitigate the effects of sleep debt and optimize alertness, strategies such as improving sleep hygiene, maintaining regular sleep schedules, and minimizing exposure to blue light from electronic devices before bedtime are recommended[^70][^71].

In summary, sleep duration plays a central role in maintaining daytime alertness, with both sleep debt and irregular sleep patterns contributing to an increase in daytime drowsiness. Effective management of sleep requires an emphasis on achieving the recommended duration of seven to nine hours nightly and ensuring consistency in sleep practices to promote better health and performance during waking hours[^1][^72][^73][^74].

## <a id="section-4"></a>4. Obstructive Sleep Apnea: A Contributor to Daytime Drowsiness

Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA) is a prevalent disorder that significantly contributes to daytime drowsiness and is characterized by repeated episodes of complete or partial upper airway obstruction during sleep. These interruptions in breathing lead to disrupted sleep patterns, often resulting in excessive daytime sleepiness due to the inability to achieve continuous, restorative sleep[^17][^18][^75][^76][^14].

The underlying mechanism of OSA involves the relaxation of the throat muscles during sleep, which causes airway collapse, leading to brief awakenings and periods of halted breathing known as apneas or hypopneas[^10][^77][^78]. Common symptoms include loud snoring, choking, or gasping for air, and individuals with OSA often wake feeling unrefreshed despite having an adequate amount of sleep[^43][^79][^80][^77]. This fragmented sleep contributes to daytime drowsiness, cognitive impairments, and difficulty maintaining alertness during daily activities[^81][^82][^7].

OSA is associated with significant health risks, including an increased likelihood of cardiovascular diseases, diabetes, hypertension, and stroke—a result of frequent nightly oxygen desaturation and increased sympathetic nervous system activity due to repeated arousals[^83][^84][^85][^86][^87]. Individuals with severe OSA may experience up to hundreds of apneic episodes per night, exacerbating the physiological stress on the body[^88][^83][^85].

Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) therapy is the most common treatment for OSA. It involves wearing a mask over the nose and/or mouth while sleeping, which provides a constant stream of air that keeps the airway open[^18][^76][^43][^89]. CPAP has been shown to reduce apneic events, improve sleep quality, decrease daytime sleepiness, and mitigate some of the associated health comorbidities[^18][^90][^91][^55]. However, adherence to CPAP can be challenging, and alternative treatments such as oral appliances, weight management, and lifestyle modifications are explored for those unable or unwilling to use CPAP[^92][^93][^94].

Moreover, there are potential impacts of OSA on cognitive function. The intermittent hypoxia resulting from repeated apneas can damage brain structures, compromise cognitive abilities, and potentially accelerate age-related cognitive decline or exacerbate existing cognitive disorders[^95][^96][^97]. The cognitive deficits often reported in individuals with OSA underscore the necessity of effective diagnosis and management[^98][^99][^100].

In summary, obstructive sleep apnea is a significant health issue linked closely to daytime drowsiness and various long-term health consequences. Understanding its impact on sleep quality and overall health is critical for devising strategies to mitigate its effects and improve the life quality of those affected[^18][^75][^10][^101][^102].

## <a id="section-7"></a>7. Improving Sleep Hygiene for Achieving Better Nighttime Rest

Avoiding exposure to screens and electronic devices before bedtime is crucial, as the blue light emitted from these devices can suppress melatonin production and disrupt the natural sleep-wake cycle[^125][^146][^147][^148][^48]. Regular exercise is beneficial for sleep but should be scheduled appropriately, as vigorous exercise too close to bedtime may inhibit the ability to fall asleep[^152][^153]. Addressing stress and anxiety through mindfulness practices or cognitive-behavioral therapy can also support better sleep quality. ## <a id="section-8"></a>8. Circadian Rhythm Management and Its Effect on Sleep Quality

Circadian rhythm management is pivotal in ensuring optimal sleep quality and overall health. Circadian rhythms are internal biological clocks that operate on a roughly 24-hour cycle, influencing a wide range of functions such as the sleep-wake cycle, hormone release, and metabolism. These rhythms are primarily synchronized by environmental cues, including light and temperature, which impact melatonin secretion and subsequently regulate sleep patterns[^70][^32][^52].

Managing circadian rhythms involves aligning one's sleep schedule with these natural cycles to improve sleep quality. Consistency in sleep-wake timing is crucial, as regularity helps reinforce the body's internal clock, facilitating more restful sleep and reducing the likelihood of disruptions. Strategies for effective management include maintaining a stable sleep and wake time across all days, minimizing exposure to artificial light—particularly blue light—from screens during evening hours, and enhancing exposure to natural light during the day to help reset the biological clock[^162][^163][^6].

Moreover, disruption of circadian rhythms is linked to various health issues, including metabolic dysfunction, cognitive impairments, and mood disorders. For instance, irregular sleep patterns have been associated with increased risks of obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular diseases, and mood disturbances such as depression and anxiety. Such disruptions can exacerbate sleep problems, leading to excessive daytime sleepiness and reduced productivity[^164][^68][^147][^165][^166].

Light exposure is a critical factor in regulating circadian rhythms. Bright light exposure during the day supports alertness and cognitive function, while exposure to dim or blue-toned light in the evening can align rhythms conducive to rest. Using amber-tinted glasses to filter blue light or engaging in daytime activities that maximize natural light exposure can greatly enhance sleep quality by supporting circadian alignment[^163][^167][^123][^168].

Additionally, dietary habits have a role in managing circadian rhythms. Eating at irregular times can disrupt these biological clocks, contributing to poor sleep quality and increased daytime sleepiness. Aligning meal times with daylight hours, focusing on nutritious foods that support circadian health, and avoiding heavy meals and substances like caffeine close to bedtime may assist in maintaining a balanced rhythm[^169][^170][^171][^172][^106].

For individuals experiencing significant circadian rhythm dysregulation, interventions such as cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I), light therapy, and when necessary, melatonin supplements are recognized as effective. These interventions aim to recalibrate the internal clock and restore healthy sleep patterns, ultimately improving sleep quality and reducing dependence on daytime naps[^164][^9][^141][^69][^13].

In conclusion, effective management of circadian rhythms is fundamental to achieving better sleep quality and overall health. By incorporating regular sleep schedules, optimizing light exposure, managing meal timings, and considering scientifically supported interventions, individuals can significantly enhance their sleep patterns and daytime alertness[^162][^163][^6][^168][^172][^106].

## <a id="section-9"></a>9. Medical Conditions Known to Cause Daytime Sleepiness

Numerous medical conditions can cause daytime sleepiness, and recognizing these is crucial to managing symptoms effectively. Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) is a leading cause of excessive daytime sleepiness, marked by episodes of breathing cessation during sleep that disrupt normal sleep architecture and reduce the restorative effects of sleep. OSA is linked with various health conditions such as cardiovascular issues, diabetes, and increased mortality risk due to its impact on oxygenation and sleep quality[^173][^75][^17][^174][^175].

Idiopathic hypersomnia is another sleep disorder that leads to significant daytime sleepiness despite adequate or prolonged nighttime sleep. This condition results in excessive sleepiness and difficulty waking up, impacting daily functioning and quality of life[^176]. In narcolepsy, a disorder involving a deficiency in hypocretin, individuals experience uncontrollable sleep attacks and disrupted nighttime sleep leading to daytime drowsiness[^23][^177][^178]. This autoimmune disorder triggers the immune system to attack neurons producing hypocretin, resulting in an inability to regulate the sleep-wake cycle[^177].

Restless legs syndrome (RLS) is characterized by an irresistible urge to move the legs, particularly at night, disrupting sleep and contributing to daytime fatigue. It is commonly linked with poor sleep quality and can be exacerbated by underlying conditions such as kidney disease and cardiovascular problems[^179][^180].

In addition, certain neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer's and Parkinson's carry a strong association with sleep disturbances, including reduced REM sleep and increased sleep fragmentation. These disruptions can precede cognitive decline and are often very prevalent in these conditions[^181][^182].

Neurological conditions such as traumatic brain injuries and concussions can also lead to sleep problems that persist post-recovery, resulting in ongoing daytime sleepiness[^183][^184]. People with chronic pain or conditions like fibromyalgia often report non-restorative sleep, leading to significant daytime drowsiness[^185].

Psychiatric disorders, including depression and anxiety, are frequently associated with insomnia and hypersomnia, disrupting sleep continuity and adversely affecting wakefulness[^25][^186][^187]. Similarly, certain medications, psychiatric and otherwise, may exacerbate or induce sleep disorders, increasing daytime sleepiness as a side effect[^188][^189].

Excessive daytime sleepiness can be influenced by metabolic disorders such as diabetes, where sleep apnea often co-occurs and exacerbates sleepiness due to fluctuating blood sugar levels affecting energy balance[^91][^190]. This bidirectional relationship underscores the intricate ties between endocrine health and sleep quality[^191]. 

Recognizing these medical conditions is vital for diagnosing and managing daytime sleepiness appropriately, enabling affected individuals to achieve better functioning and quality of life through targeted interventions.

## <a id="section-10"></a>10. Current Medical and Non-Medical Treatments Available for Excessive Daytime Sleepiness

Excessive daytime sleepiness (EDS) can be addressed through a range of current medical and non-medical treatment options available to patients experiencing this condition, which often results from underlying sleep disorders like obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) or insomnia. Medical treatments frequently involve pharmacological interventions tailored to manage EDS directly or through the treatment of underlying conditions.

One of the primary medical treatments for EDS, especially related to OSA, is Continuous Positive Airway Pressure (CPAP) therapy. CPAP is highly effective in maintaining open airways during sleep, thereby improving sleep quality and reducing daytime drowsiness in patients with OSA. Research underscores CPAP's ability to alleviate symptoms of daytime sleepiness, with adherence being crucial for effectiveness. Studies like those by various sleep medicine researchers suggest that consistent use of CPAP can improve overall patient outcomes, including reduced cardiovascular risk, improved cognitive function, and enhanced quality of life[^192][^193][^194].

Apart from CPAP, newer pharmacologic treatments have emerged. Medication such as modafinil and armodafinil are wakefulness-promoting agents prescribed to those with EDS stemming from conditions like narcolepsy and sleep apnea. Modafinil has demonstrated cognitive benefits, including improved decision-making and alertness, which are crucial for individuals experiencing significant daytime sleepiness[^195][^196]. In cases of narcolepsy, medications targeting orexin receptors, essential for maintaining wakefulness, have shown efficacy in reducing EDS and addressing symptoms like cataplexy[^197][^198]. Although some medications carry risks, including hepatotoxic effects and risks of sleep behavior disturbances, careful clinical evaluation and monitoring can help mitigate these concerns[^197][^199].

Non-pharmacological therapies also play a vital role. Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia (CBT-I) is one of the best-established non-drug approaches to treat sleep disorders contributing to EDS. CBT-I addresses maladaptive sleep habits and thoughts that contribute to sleep issues, promoting better sleep practices[^161][^124][^200]. This therapy can be delivered effectively through traditional face-to-face interactions or digital platforms, thereby increasing accessibility[^201][^202].

Supplemental interventions, such as melatonin for its sleep-regulating properties, can benefit individuals struggling with circadian rhythm disorders or needing to correct sleep-wake cycles. Light therapy has also gained traction as an adjunct treatment, as it can align circadian rhythms and improve alertness, particularly in those with Seasonal Affective Disorder (SAD)[^203][^204].

Additionally, lifestyle changes, including proper sleep hygiene, regular physical activity, and dietary adjustments, are critical. These measures not only improve sleep quality but also enhance daily energy levels[^205][^206]. Hydration control, limiting caffeine and alcohol intake, and ensuring a conducive sleep environment can further benefit individuals managing EDS. Addressing comorbidities such as obesity or metabolic health issues is also crucial, linking weight management and dietary habits to reduced sleep disturbances and improved alertness during the day[^21][^22].

Ultimately, a blended approach that combines medical and lifestyle interventions can provide effective management for excessive daytime sleepiness, helping individuals achieve better rest and alertness during waking hours. These treatments are best tailored through consultation with healthcare professionals to align with the specific needs of individuals based on their underlying health conditions and personal circumstances[^161][^207][^208].

## <a id="section-11"></a>11. Long Naps and Their Impact on Health

Long naps, defined as those lasting more than 30 minutes, have strong associations with a variety of health implications. Research consistently links long naps with increased risks of metabolic syndrome, obesity, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, atrial fibrillation, and stroke. A study involving over 300,000 participants found that napping longer than an hour can increase the risk of cardiovascular issues by up to 82% and is associated with a 46% increase in diabetes risk[^33][^209][^210][^211].

Additionally, prolonged napping may contribute to disrupted circadian rhythms, sleep-wake cycle irregularities, and overall reduced sleep quality at night. This can lead to a reliance on naps as a compensatory mechanism for insufficient nighttime sleep, which perpetuates a cycle of tiredness and inadequate sleep. Long naps can result in sleep inertia, manifesting as grogginess and impaired cognitive performance upon waking, which may last longer compared to shorter naps[^134][^212].

Interestingly, there is a biological underpinning to the inclination to take naps, with certain gene regions influencing napping behavior. This suggests that genetic factors may predispose individuals to daytime napping[^213]. While short naps under 30 minutes can have restorative benefits without interfering with nighttime sleep, longer naps have been found to potentially exacerbate health risks due to their impact on sleep patterns[^1][^73].

Furthermore, habitual long napping is correlated with negative health outcomes among older adults, particularly in the context of cognitive decline and Alzheimer’s disease. Research suggests that individuals who rely on long naps may have increased levels of tau protein, associated with cognitive impairment and Alzheimer's pathways[^153][^214]. The disruption of normal sleep processes and the increased need for longer daytime sleep may serve as indicators of underlying sleep disorders, such as sleep apnea, which further underscores the importance of addressing the root causes rather than relying solely on napping[^215][^10].

While there are contexts in which naps can provide temporary relief from sleep deprivation, their effectiveness decreases when they become a regular necessity. Improving sleep hygiene and achieving adequate nighttime rest is crucial for reducing reliance on long naps, thus minimizing associated health risks. Healthcare providers often recommend addressing underlying sleep problems, improving diet, and regular physical activity as strategies to enhance sleep quality and reduce excessive daytime sleepiness, thereby lessening the need for long naps[^216][^217].

In conclusion, while long naps can provide immediate relief from daytime drowsiness, they should not substitute for high-quality nighttime sleep due to their potential negative health impacts. Managing nap duration and enhancing overall sleep hygiene are key to optimizing health and reducing the risks associated with excessive daytime sleep[^63][^125][^218][^219].

Addressing these factors through consistent sleep patterns, early diagnosis, and effective management of sleep disorders could help mitigate the metabolic health risks linked to poor sleep quality. Continued research is necessary to refine intervention strategies and understand the underlying mechanisms better to alleviate the dual burden of sleep and metabolic health issues[^223][^33][^210][^70][^224].

## <a id="section-13"></a>13. Innovative Research and Potential New Treatments for Sleep Disorders

Recent innovations in sleep disorder research have opened up promising avenues for potential new treatments, reflecting the growing understanding of sleep's intricate mechanisms and its impact on overall health. A particularly innovative approach is the development of a wireless monitoring patch system designed to diagnose obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) at home, enabling accurate monitoring without the cumbersome equipment traditionally used in sleep labs. This device, showing an 88.5% accuracy rate, symbolizes significant advancements in making sleep disorder diagnostics more accessible and comfortable for patients[^149].

Additionally, time-restricted feeding has emerged as a lifestyle intervention that can help mitigate excessive daytime sleepiness, especially in individuals with obesity driven by high-fat diets. Research into this approach highlights the potential for non-pharmacological strategies in managing sleep-related symptoms by aligning feeding times with circadian rhythms to correct dysfunctional sleep-wake cycles[^109].

The discovery of a novel compound, YNT-185, an orexin receptor agonist, marks a breakthrough in treating conditions like narcolepsy by promoting wakefulness effectively in animal models. This advancement bypasses the challenge of delivering orexin across the blood-brain barrier, illustrating a promising path forward for addressing excessive daytime drowsiness and related disorders[^231].

Another innovative strategy seeks to treat obstructive sleep apnea through pharmacological means. Research into the nasal spray containing a potassium channel blocker demonstrated its efficacy in reducing throat collapses during sleep, offering an alternative for those who cannot tolerate CPAP therapy[^232].

These innovations reflect an interdisciplinary focus, integrating technology, pharmacology, and lifestyle modifications. Continued exploration and refinement of these potential treatments could offer significant improvements in the quality of life for individuals suffering from sleep disorders, reducing the burdens of daytime sleepiness and enhancing overall well-being.

## <a id="section-14"></a>14. Conclusion: Recommendations and Strategies for Reducing Daytime Drowsiness

Reducing daytime drowsiness requires a multi-pronged approach focusing on improving overall sleep quality, addressing lifestyle factors, and implementing specific strategies confirmed by research. Enhancing sleep hygiene, maintaining a consistent sleep schedule, and creating a sleeping environment that is dark, quiet, and cool are foundational steps that foster restorative nighttime sleep. Regular physical activity, particularly aerobic exercise, is beneficial, as it not only improves sleep quality but also enhances overall health and well-being[^233].

For those suffering from sleep disorders such as obstructive sleep apnea (OSA), continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) therapy is a well-documented treatment that enhances sleep quality and reduces excessive daytime sleepiness[^234]. Oral appliances, like mandibular advancement devices, offer an alternative for those who cannot tolerate CPAP, notably in cases of mild to moderate OSA[^92].

Napping strategies can play a significant role in mitigating daytime sleepiness. While brief, scheduled naps may help alleviate acute sleep deficits, overly long naps (more than 40 minutes) can exacerbate health risks and should be avoided[^211]. It's crucial to ensure that napping does not become a primary strategy for addressing chronic sleep insufficiency[^222].

Diet and lifestyle modifications support better sleep health by promoting balanced eating patterns, avoiding stimulants before bed, and maintaining regular mealtimes to align with circadian rhythms[^235][^236]. Moreover, addressing stress through relaxation techniques, mindfulness, and cognitive-behavioral therapy can enhance sleep and reduce the impacts of stress-induced sleep disturbances[^237].

Emerging technologies, including wearable devices and mobile health applications, offer innovative means to track sleep patterns, promote better adherence to sleep-related treatments, and provide personalized sleep scheduling advice[^238][^239]. These tools, along with personalized sleep interventions and potentially new treatment options like orexin receptor antagonists, are areas of promising research that could offer more targeted strategies for improving sleep and related daytime alertness[^231].

Overall, adopting a comprehensive strategy tailored to individual needs that encompasses improving sleep quality, effectively managing sleep disorders, and maintaining consistent sleep habits is essential in reducing daytime drowsiness and enhancing overall functioning[^240].
